Liquors

Candy is dandy, but liquor is quicker- especially during quarantine! Liquor is made from the fermentation of grain and other plants. There are six categories of liquor: vodka, tequila, gin, whiskey, rum, and brandy. You may have heard the terms "top shelf" and "bottom shelf" when referring to liquor- think about when you're at the ABC store walking through the aisles. The cheap stuff is on the bottom shelf! However, just because something is cheap doesn't necessarily mean it's bad. Provided below are recommended options at several price points within each category of liquor.

Image at right courtesy of Marc Tarlock on Flickr, CC BY-SA 2.0.


Vodka

Vodka was originally made from fermented potatoes, although it is now usually made with fermented grains.
  • Smirnoff - if you're on a budget, Smirnoff is the best of the inexpensive brands.
  • Tito's Handmade Vodka - gluten-free and made in the USA!
  • Ketel One - even though there are more expensive brands out there, I consider Ketel One to be the best.
    • Check out Ketel One Botanicals for flavored vodka! I love the grapefruit-rose one.

Tequila

Tequila is made from fermented agave. The great thing about tequila is that YOU DO NOT NEED TO SPEND TONS OF MONEY TO GET A GREAT TEQUILA. As long as you look for a label that says 100% agave, you're good! There are three types of tequila: silver or blanco, reposado, and añejo. Each type of tequila is aged for a different amount of time, with añejo being aged for up to three years. Reposado and añejo tequilas are smoother and more flavorful than silver tequilas.
  • Sauza - this is my all-time favorite tequila!
  • Lunazul - another great inexpensive option!
  • Espolon - this bottle would look great on your bar cart!
  • Casamigos - George Clooney's brand

Gin

Gin is made from adding distilled juniper berries to fermented grain. The juniper flavor is more pronounced in some gins than others. Some gins can also taste more like citrus.
  • Beefeater - a dry gin (meaning no sweetness is added)
  • Hendrick's - simply unbeatable. It's more botanical-tasting than Beefeater.

Image at right courtesy of Larry Hoffman on Flickr, CC BY-SA 2.0.


Whiskey

Whiskey is made from fermented grains like corn, rye, and barley. There are five categories of whiskeys: Scotch, Bourbon, Irish Whiskey, Canadian Whiskey, and Japanese Whiskey. (Note: we won't be dealing with Scotch at all here- Scotch is not a typical cocktail ingredient because it is enjoyed neat or on the rocks.)
  • Maker's Mark
  • Mellow Corn
  • Woodford Reserve
  • Suntory Whisky Toki
  • Jameson - As a service industry worker, I would be remiss if I did not mention Jameson, an iconic Irish whiskey enjoyed by approximately every line cook immediately after clocking out.

Rum

Rum is made from fermented and distilled sugar cane. Like tequila, there are different ages of rum, with the darker rums being smoother and more richly flavored.
  • Castillo - great inexpensive option
  • Gosling's Black Seal - well-known Bermudan rum
  • Ron Zacapa 23-Year - delicious, rich rum for sipping

Image at right courtesy of mobil'homme on Flickr, CC BY-SA 2.0.


Brandy

Brandy is made from fermented fruit.
  • Laird's Applejack
  • Hennessy VS
  • Pisco Portón - a type of brandy from Peru

Liqueurs

A liqueur is a sweetened, distilled spirit, that usually (but not always!) has a lower alcohol content than liquor. Liqueurs (sometimes called cordials) can be crucial elements of a good cocktail because they add flavor and sweetness!

Image at right courtesy of Krista on Flickr, CC BY-SA 2.0.


Orange liqueurs:

Triple Sec - typically inexpensive (there are many brands, I like Dekuyper) and a key ingredient in a margarita. But if you feel like splurging...

  • Cointreau - smoother and higher proof than triple sec!
    • My grandma taught me this tip: soak peaches in Cointreau for a boozy summer snack!
  • Aperol - an orange liqueur that's bitter on its own but fabulous in a wine cocktail!

Other fruit liqueurs:

  • Pama - pomegranate liqueur
  • St. Germain - elderflower liqueur (here's a cheaper version!)
  • Chambord - black raspberry liqueur

Image at right courtesy of Chris Goldberg on Flickr, CC BY-SA 2.0.


Cream liqueurs:

  • The iconic Bailey's Irish Cream - sip it on the rocks or add it to your coffee! Here's a cheaper one.
  • Kahlua - coffee liqueur
  • RumChata - cinnamon liqueur

Nutty liqueurs:

  • Frangelico - hazelnut liqueur
  • Amaretto - almond liqueur. Here's a higher-end one, here's a more inexpensive one.
  • Licor 43 - vanilla liqueur

Mixers

For the best home bar setup, it's important to have the right mixers on-hand. Fresh citrus (limes, lemons, oranges, grapefruit) is necessary in most cocktails for their juices, and can always be used as garnishes if you want to feel fancy- and why shouldn't you? (If you don't like juicing citrus, I don't blame you. You can always use storebought juice, just be aware that some brands, like Rose's, have added sweetener.) Other fruits and veggies that are used in several common cocktails include cherries, jalapeño peppers (or other chilies), and cucumbers. Some of my favorite cocktails include herbs like mint and basil- consider getting some plants so you'll never run out!

Club soda, ginger beer, and tonic water are also a must-have: if you don't feel like whipping up a cocktail, you can add those ingredients to a glass with a shot of pretty much any liquor for a quick drink. (That's called a highball- drinks like a gin & tonic contain one shot of liquor in a small glass, with ice and equal proportions of a mixer. You can mix a lot of liquor with Coke too!) It's also good to have cranberry juice, orange juice, and (especially if you love Bloody Mary's) tomato juice.

Image at right courtesy of FTD Flowers on Flickr, CC BY-SA 2.0.


Bitters

Adding bitters to a cocktail can really elevate the flavor! You usually don't need more than a few drops, so one little bottle can last a long time.

  • Angostura bitters
  • Fee Brothers aromatic bitters

Sweeteners

  • Simple syrup: simple syrup is basically hummingbird feed for humans. It's equal parts sugar and hot water- put the sugar in a jar, add the water, and stir until all the sugar is dissolved. Let it cool.
  • Agave nectar: if you're conscious of your sugar intake, agave is a wonderful substitute.

Drink Responsibly!
